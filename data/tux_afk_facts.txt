It was almost called "Freax": Linus Torvalds originally wanted to name his project "Freax" (a combination of "free," "freak," and "x" to denote a Unix-like system). However, the administrator of the FTP server where Linus first uploaded the code, Ari Lemmke, did not like the name and created a directory called "linux" instead. The name stuck.

Tux the penguin has a backstory: The official mascot of Linux is a slightly chubby penguin named Tux. Linus Torvalds chose a penguin after he was bitten by a fairy penguin while visiting a zoo in Australia in 1993.

Linux dominates supercomputers: While desktop adoption is relatively low compared to Windows or macOS, Linux is the undisputed king of heavy computing. Since 2017, 100% of the world's top 500 supercomputers run on Linux.

Steve Jobs tried to hire Linus Torvalds: In the year 2000, Steve Jobs invited Linus Torvalds to Apple's campus and offered him a job to drop Linux and work on macOS. Torvalds declined the offer, preferring to keep working on his open-source project.

Linux is in space: The International Space Station (ISS) switched from Windows to Linux in 2013 because they needed an operating system that was stable and reliable. NASA's Ingenuity helicopter, which successfully flew on Mars, also runs on a Linux operating system.

Microsoft contributes heavily to Linux: In 2001, former Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer infamously called Linux a "cancer." Today, Microsoft is a platinum member of the Linux Foundation, one of the top contributors to the Linux kernel, and has integrated Linux natively into Windows via WSL.

Linux powers your phone: If you use an Android smartphone, you are using Linux. The Android operating system is built on a modified version of the Linux kernel.

Asteroids are named after it: In 1996, astronomers discovered two asteroids and decided to name them after the open-source phenomenon. Asteroid 9793 is named "Torvalds," and asteroid 9885 is named "Linux."

It has over 30 million lines of code: The Linux kernel is massive and constantly growing. It currently contains well over 30 million lines of code, with thousands of developers from all over the world contributing every year.

Linus only wrote a tiny fraction of it: Even though Linus Torvalds created Linux, his actual code makes up less than 1% of the current kernel. Today, his primary role is managing and merging the code submitted by other developers.

Linux Birthed Git: Linus Torvalds didn't just create the most widely used operating system kernel; he also created Git, the world's most popular version control system. He built it in 2005 over a weekend because he was frustrated with existing tools for managing the massive Linux kernel codebase.

Hollywood Runs on Linux: If you love CGI-heavy blockbuster movies, you have Linux to thank. Over 90% of Hollywood's visual effects are rendered on Linux machines. The adoption started way back with Titanic (1997), and studios like DreamWorks, Pixar, and Weta Digital rely heavily on it.

It Powers the Large Hadron Collider: CERN's Large Hadron Collider (LHC), the most powerful particle accelerator in the world, uses a customized Linux distribution (formerly Scientific Linux, now AlmaLinux/Rocky Linux) to process and analyze the mind-boggling amounts of data generated by particle collisions.

The "GNU/Linux" Debate: Technically, "Linux" is just the kernel—the core that talks to the hardware. The rest of the operating system (the interface, utilities, and commands) comes largely from the GNU Project. Because of this, free software advocate Richard Stallman famously insists it should be called "GNU/Linux."

Nuclear Submarines Use It: The US Navy uses Linux systems on its nuclear submarines. They transitioned from proprietary systems to Linux to improve security, reliability, and cost-efficiency.

It Speaks Two Languages Now: For over 30 years, the Linux kernel was written almost entirely in the C programming language (with a little bit of Assembly). However, in 2022, the kernel officially adopted Rust as its second accepted language to help improve memory safety and prevent bugs.

The First Commercial Distro Was a Tree: The first commercial Linux distribution was released in 1992 and was called Yggdrasil (named after the mythical World Tree in Norse cosmology). It was also one of the first distros to be distributed on a CD-ROM rather than floppy disks.

Bullet Trains Rely on It: Japan's world-famous, ultra-fast bullet trains (the Shinkansen) use a Linux-based system to manage their incredibly precise traffic control and scheduling networks.

Linus Has a "Walking Desk": Linus Torvalds is famous for his unique home office setup. He works from a treadmill desk, walking at a slow pace while reviewing code and managing the kernel.

The Kernel Has Over 30,000 Contributors: Since its inception in 1991, more than 30,000 different developers have contributed code to the Linux kernel. It is arguably the largest and most successful collaborative engineering project in human history.
